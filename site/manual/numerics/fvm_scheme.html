The Finite Volume Method

The finite volume method solves the PDE by pairing the solution grid points with control volumes around them. This allows us to integrate the PDE system by volume, converting the flux divergences to flux surface integrals.

Therfore the PDE of the \(l\) element of the generic state vector \(\boldsymbol q\)

\[ \frac{\partial q^l}{\partial t} + \boldsymbol \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol F_{\rm adv}^l + \boldsymbol \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol F_{\rm diff}^l = S^l\]

will become

\[ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} q^j\int_V {\rm d} V+ \oint_A \boldsymbol F_{\rm adv}^j {\rm d} \boldsymbol A + \oint \boldsymbol F_{\rm diff}^j {\rm d} \boldsymbol A = \int_V S^j {\rm d} V. \]

Introducing the \(\Delta V_{i,j,k} =\Delta x_i \Delta y_j \Delta z_k\) finite control volume (in general the $dV_{i,j,k} volume element on a curviliear grid), We define the cell average as

\[ \overline{ q}_{i,j,k}^l = \frac{ \int_{\Delta V} q_{i,j,k}^l(\boldsymbol r) dV}{ \int_{\Delta V} dV}\approx \frac{1}{\Delta V_{i,j,k}} \int_{\Delta V} q_{i,j,k}^l(\boldsymbol r) dV. \]
